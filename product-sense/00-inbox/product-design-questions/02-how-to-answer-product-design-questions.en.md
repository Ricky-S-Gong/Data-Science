# How to Answer Product Design Questions

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Feasible product | A product that can realistically solve a user problem in a way that makes sense for the company and that users will like. |
| Product vision | A memorable statement of the future product experience and why it matters. |
| Pain point | A concrete obstacle that prevents users from achieving a goal. |
| Opportunity area | A point of friction or unmet need where the product can create value, even if users are not fully blocked. |
| Moonshot | A bold solution that goes beyond incremental improvement and tries to solve the root problem in a transformative way. |
| 5 Whys | A technique for repeatedly asking “why” to uncover the deeper root cause of a problem. |

## What Product Design Questions Ask

Product design questions ask you to design or improve a product.

Common question types include:

- Design a product: “Design an app for job-seekers to match with employers.”
- Improve a product: “Improve Headspace.”
- Design for a specific audience: “Design a vending machine for the blind.”
- Moonshots: a Google favorite, such as “Design X, assuming you have infinite resources.”

Overall, your goal is to come up with a feasible product given the constraints of the question.

In this context, feasible means:

- The product solves a real user problem.
- The solution makes sense for the company.
- Users would like or adopt the solution.
- The idea is concrete enough to evaluate.
- The tradeoffs are realistic.

These questions typically take about 25 minutes, excluding follow-ups. Interviewers often ask product design questions early to filter candidates who lack essential product skills, but they are also common in on-site interviews. In a standard PM interview loop, expect to answer several.

## The 7-Step Framework

A solid process is to clarify the problem, gather relevant context, understand users and their challenges, explore solutions, choose the best option, and justify your answer.

![Product design seven-step framework](../../assets/images/product-design-questions/framework/seven-step-framework.svg)

| Step | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Clarify and get context | Understand the problem space, constraints, and strategic context. |
| 2. Define users | Segment users and choose an interesting user subset. |
| 3. Identify pain points and opportunity areas | Understand user goals and what blocks or frustrates them. |
| 4. Brainstorm possible solutions | Generate multiple solutions tied to the chosen pain point. |
| 5. Define a product vision | Turn the strongest idea into a memorable long-term direction. |
| 6. Prioritize features | Choose features that best support the vision and user journey. |
| 7. Evaluate and recap | Summarize the design, tradeoffs, risks, and next steps. |

## Example Prompt

Let’s work through the example:

> Design a better gym experience.

## Step 1: Clarify And Get Context

First, clarify any vagueness in the problem. After clarifying ambiguous company names and keywords, gather context that helps you understand the problem space and define strategic considerations that might influence the design.

Useful clarification questions include:

- What do you mean by “gym experience”?
- What does “better” mean?
- Are there specific metrics we are trying to drive?
- What is the timeline?
- Are there constraints I should know about?
- Am I improving a home gym product or a commercial gym?
- Am I improving one particular gym location or a chain?
- Am I improving a specific gym like Planet Fitness, or making general improvements?

The interviewer may give a vague answer. If that happens, make assumptions and state them clearly so the interviewer can correct you.

#### Example Callout

For “Design a better gym experience,” assume the interviewer says:

- “Make whatever assumptions you like.”
- “We just know that the gym experience works for some users and not others.”
- “We want to make it better for more users.”

You can state:

> Going forward, I’ll answer the question assuming we are improving a generic commercial gym.

![Gym example whiteboard 1](../../assets/images/product-design-questions/gym-example/whiteboard-1.svg)

## Step 2: Define Users

Next, define subsets of users that partition the total user base, then choose an interesting subset for a deeper dive.

Common segmentation methods include:

- Demographics, such as age or income.
- Behavioral characteristics, such as usage frequency or target actions.
- Geographic location.
- Customer maturity, such as new, casual, power, or churn-risk users.
- Motivation, such as social, health, productivity, or entertainment goals.

A great answer segments users in a way that is meaningful for the question. A helpful tip is to segment users who may have very different needs.

After listing user segments, pick one and explain why it is valuable to discuss. Consider:

- Strategic value, such as early adopters or high-growth users.
- Scale of impact.
- Depth of user pain.
- How well the company can address the segment.

#### Example Callout

For the gym example, segment users by gym usage:

- Pro users.
- Novice users.
- Users who only take classes.
- Users who have memberships but do not go to the gym.

Target segment:

> Users who have memberships but do not go to the gym stand out because they are clearly experiencing an obstacle. They were motivated enough to join and pay, but something prevents them from going. If we solve this issue, we may create value for other gym-goers and attract new members.

![Gym example whiteboard 2](../../assets/images/product-design-questions/gym-example/whiteboard-2.svg)

## Step 3: Identify Pain Points And Opportunity Areas

Consider the goals your chosen users may have.

Explicit obstacles are pain points. If nothing fully blocks users from achieving their goals but friction exists, you have found an opportunity area.

Take an empathetic position. Ask:

- What are users trying to accomplish?
- Why does this goal matter to them?
- What makes the goal hard?
- Is the goal urgent?
- Are the decisions important or trivial?
- What emotional, logistical, financial, or social barriers exist?

#### Note Callout

Use the “broad then deep” mini-framework:

- Broad: generate a wide list of possible user goals, pains, and frictions.
- Deep: choose the most important pain point and analyze it in detail.

This demonstrates user empathy and avoids prematurely anchoring on one obvious answer.

#### Example Callout

Absent gym members likely bought memberships for goals such as:

- Getting healthier.
- Looking better.
- Gaining confidence.
- Making friends or being social.

Possible barriers include:

- Feeling uncomfortable or intimidated.
- Feeling bored.
- Busy schedules.
- Traffic.
- Inconvenient gym location.

You might choose emotional hurdles rather than logistical hurdles because logistics differ by user and may be harder to solve broadly.

Selected pain point:

> Users may feel bored at the gym, especially during peak hours when they are waiting for popular equipment.

![Gym example whiteboard 3](../../assets/images/product-design-questions/gym-example/whiteboard-3.svg)

## Step 4: Brainstorm Possible Solutions

Now brainstorm ideas that could solve the selected pain point.

Generate at least three solid ideas before moving on. It is fine to brainstorm more and eliminate weak ideas.

Check each idea against the pain point and do not be afraid to be creative. Interviewers want to see excitement and passion for product.

One helpful technique is to think of products you like, especially products solving a similar problem in another context.

For the gym example, Duolingo is a useful analogy:

- It helps people improve themselves.
- It removes motivation and routine barriers.
- It is fun and easy to use.
- It emphasizes consistency rather than intensity.

Those characteristics can inspire a better gym design.

#### Example Callout

For users who feel bored while waiting for equipment, possible solutions include:

- A workout recommendation system that accounts for peak hours.
- Gamifying the gym experience so users feel more engaged overall.
- Activities designed specifically for the time between sets.

![Gym example whiteboard 4](../../assets/images/product-design-questions/gym-example/whiteboard-4.svg)

## Step 5: Define A Forward-Looking Product Vision

Pick the strongest solution and imagine what the product could become in five or ten years.

Create a short tagline that captures the point you are making. You want the interviewer to remember it when they score the interview.

Write the vision on the whiteboard if you are using one, then refer back to it as you continue.

#### Example Callout

A forward-looking gym vision:

> A gym visit is made up of more than active workout time. Non-active time is often awkward or boring. In a futuristic gym, that time can be fun and exciting.

A concise product vision:

> Let’s build a gym that offers engaging activities between sets, helping users stay in the right mindset while getting a more effective workout.

![Gym example whiteboard 5](../../assets/images/product-design-questions/gym-example/whiteboard-5.svg)

## Step 6: Prioritize Features

Once you have a compelling vision, prioritize features.

Walk the interviewer through a quick user journey where the user interacts with your product. This helps you identify how the product fits into the existing flow and keeps the answer user-centric.

Then brainstorm a short feature list and prioritize according to which features best support the product vision.

Useful prioritization dimensions include:

- Scale: how many users does this help?
- Ease of expansion: can this expand to other user subsets or attract new users?
- Strategic impact: how well does this support the company vision?
- User pain fit: how directly does it solve the chosen pain point?
- Feasibility: can this realistically be built and operated?

You do not need to describe every detail. But interviewers expect you to describe what the user sees, what the user does, and how the feature delivers on the goals and vision.

#### Example Callout

For the gym example, ask:

> What is the process of waiting?

Users are usually waiting for a machine or resting between sets. The product has a short window, maybe five to ten minutes, to keep the user engaged without distracting them from the workout.

Possible features:

- Workout logging app.
- Stats tracking.
- Workout journal prompts.
- Tips and recommendations for the next set.

![Gym example whiteboard 6](../../assets/images/product-design-questions/gym-example/whiteboard-6.svg)

## Step 7: Evaluate And Recap

At the end, summarize your insights and design. Then evaluate the design and discuss next steps.

Consider:

- Tradeoffs you made.
- Alternate use cases.
- Edge cases.
- What you would do differently with more time.
- Risks.
- Implementation challenges.
- Metrics for success.

Common follow-up questions include:

- “Can you see any risks with this design?”
- “What challenges do you anticipate in implementing this product?”

Discussing risks proactively signals that your ideas are grounded in reality.

#### Example Callout

For the gym design:

> One risk is that gyms are busy places with heavy equipment, so it could be dangerous if people are too distracted. We also need to ensure this does not slow down workouts or create more bottlenecks.

## Tips For Product Design Questions

### Consider Company Strategy

Strategic concerns help you reach a broader and deeper understanding of the problem.

Prompts:

- Company mission: What is the company mission? Why does the company care about this space? How could this product support the mission?
- Company strategy: What strategic goals might the company have? How could this product support those goals or open a new strategic direction?
- Market understanding: What alternatives exist? Where are the gaps? What is valuable in this market?

### Ace Moonshot Questions By Abstracting The Problem

Moonshots are bold ideas that go beyond incremental change. They are common in Google interviews, but you can “moonshot” any design question by digging to the root of an important problem.

You are on the right track if your solution solves the problem completely and simply for the user. If you can eliminate the conditions that cause the problem, even better.

Two helpful strategies:

1. Treat the product as a black box.

The input is the user’s current state. The output is the user as they want to be. Remove assumptions about how the user gets from current state to desired state, then explore transformative options.

2. Use the 5 Whys.

Ask “why” repeatedly until you uncover the root problem.

For example, if asked to build an alarm clock for deaf users, the real user goal is not “use an alarm clock.” The goal is “wake up at a certain time.” That framing opens up options beyond modifying a traditional alarm clock.

### Improve Existing Products With Existing Users In Mind

Questions like “Improve Instagram’s home tab” are similar to design questions, but you must consider:

- Existing users.
- Existing product goals.
- Current user expectations.
- Market dynamics.
- Strategic opportunities.

Common improvement directions include:

- Addressing underserved users.
- Supporting new use cases.
- Adapting the product to open new strategic opportunities.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It Hurts | Better Move |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Jumping straight to a solution | The interviewer cannot see how you think or why your idea is good. | Explain your reasoning and keep an open mind while exploring. |
| Forgetting to segment users | Broad users hide meaningful needs. | Even if given a constraint, segment further. For “Gmail for kids,” choose an age range or school level. |
| Trying to design for everybody | You end up with something nobody deeply cares about. | Narrow the scope so user insights become meaningful. |
| Checking off framework boxes | The answer feels mechanical and may miss insight. | Use the framework as guidance, not a checklist. Think holistically at every stage. |

## 30-Second Summary

To answer product design questions, clarify the problem, define users, identify pain points, brainstorm solutions, create a product vision, prioritize features, and evaluate tradeoffs. In the gym example, I would clarify that we are improving a generic commercial gym, segment users by usage, focus on absent members, identify boredom and intimidation as key barriers, brainstorm solutions like customized recommendations, gamification, and between-set activities, then define a vision for a gym where inactive time is engaging and productive. Finally, I would prioritize features around the user journey and discuss risks such as distraction, safety, and workout bottlenecks.
