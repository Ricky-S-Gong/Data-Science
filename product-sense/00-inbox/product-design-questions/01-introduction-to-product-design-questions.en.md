# Introduction to Product Design Questions

## Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Product design questions | Interview questions that ask you to design, improve, or discuss a product. |
| Product Sense | A PM interview area that evaluates whether you understand users, product value, tradeoffs, strategy, and business context. |
| Design Thinking | A user-centered approach to problem solving that typically emphasizes empathy, problem definition, ideation, prototyping, and iteration. |
| User-centricity | The ability to make product decisions from a clear understanding of user needs, goals, constraints, and context. |
| Product vision | A concise statement of what the product should become and why it matters. |
| Business acumen | The ability to connect product choices to company goals, market dynamics, monetization, and strategic advantage. |

## What Product Design Questions Are

Product design questions ask you to design, improve, or discuss products.

Depending on the company, product-focused interviews may be called:

- Product Sense.
- Design Thinking.
- Product Design.
- Product Strategy and Design.
- Product Thinking.

These questions are your best opportunity to show that you can build great products that meet the needs of:

- Your users.
- Your company.
- The market.

The interviewer is not simply asking whether you can brainstorm features. They are testing whether you can discover an important user problem, understand why it matters, generate thoughtful solutions, and choose a direction that is useful, feasible, and strategically sound.

## What To Expect

The most common types of product design questions include the following.

### 1. Design A New Product

Example:

- “Design a product for users stuck in rush hour traffic.”

In this type of question, you usually start with an open-ended problem space. The challenge is to define the user, understand the context, identify a real pain point, and then design a product around it.

Strong answers avoid jumping straight to features. They first clarify who the user is and what problem is worth solving.

### 2. Improve An Existing Product

Example:

- “Improve the birthday experience on Facebook.”

Here, the product already exists. Your job is to improve a specific experience within it.

Strong answers should:

- Understand the existing product's purpose.
- Identify the relevant user groups.
- Find pain points in the current flow.
- Propose improvements that fit the product's broader ecosystem.
- Explain tradeoffs and success metrics.

### 3. Favorite Product Questions

Example:

- “What’s your favorite product?”

This question sounds casual, but it is still evaluative. The interviewer wants to know whether you can explain why a product works.

A strong answer should discuss:

- Who the product serves.
- What problem it solves.
- Why the experience is effective.
- What tradeoffs the product makes.
- How the product could be improved.

### 4. Brainstorm Product Ideas

Example:

- “Imagine Google is adding an emotion sensor to Pixel phones. Come up with different ideas for apps that could be built using this sensor.”

This type of question tests creativity, breadth, and prioritization.

Strong answers should:

- Generate a wide set of ideas.
- Group ideas into themes.
- Choose the strongest ideas based on user value, feasibility, and business fit.
- Avoid stopping at the first obvious idea.

## What This Module Will Teach

In this module, you will learn a framework to answer product questions by:

- Identifying users.
- Defining user pain points.
- Designing solutions.
- Brainstorming forward-looking product ideas.

## What Interviewers Are Looking For

Interviewers ask product design questions to assess core PM skills.

| Skill | What It Means In An Interview |
| --- | --- |
| User-centricity and empathy | You understand users as real people with goals, frustrations, context, and constraints. |
| Design skills and product vision | You can create a coherent product direction, not just a pile of features. |
| Strategic insight and business acumen | You connect product ideas to company strategy, market dynamics, and business value. |
| Ability to prioritize | You can choose the most important user problem and the most promising solution. |
| Passion and creativity | You bring energy, originality, and curiosity to ambiguous product spaces. |

To succeed, you need to rely on product intuition to solve an important user problem in a useful and feasible way.

## The Core Difference From Analytical Questions

Analytical questions focus on metrics, diagnosis, experiments, and structured reasoning with data.

Product design questions focus on:

- Users.
- Pain points.
- Product experiences.
- Creative solutions.
- Vision.
- Strategic fit.

Both question types require structure. The difference is the center of gravity:

- Analytical questions ask: “How do we measure, diagnose, or evaluate?”
- Product design questions ask: “What should we build, for whom, and why?”

## Okay, Good, And Great Answers

### Okay Answer

An okay answer identifies:

- A user segment.
- The users' problems.
- A viable solution for the most important problem.

The justifications are reasonable but surface-level.

Example pattern:

- “Commuters are frustrated in traffic, so I would build an app that recommends podcasts and alternate routes.”

This may be viable, but it does not yet show deep user understanding, strong prioritization, or strategic thought.

### Good Answer

A good answer gives a clear sense of:

- Who the users are.
- What they care about.
- Which problems matter most.
- Why the final idea is a good fit for the user.

It identifies critical problems, prioritizes the most important one, and provides multiple good solutions.

A good answer may contain elements of a great answer but does not fully tie everything together.

For example, the candidate may demonstrate a deep understanding of users but only a surface-level understanding of why their pain points exist.

### Great Answer

A great answer demonstrates deep understanding of:

- Users.
- User goals.
- User context.
- Why users' pain points exist.
- How the product fits the company's strategy.
- How the market context shapes the opportunity.

The solutions reach the core of immediate user problems while also providing long-term strategic value to the business.

At least some solutions should feel bold, but still viable and concrete.

A great answer places the design in a larger strategic and market context. It shows not only what should be built, but why this is the right direction for the user, the business, and the product's future.

## Comparison Table

| Answer Level | User Understanding | Problem Prioritization | Solution Quality | Strategic Context |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Okay | Identifies a user segment and basic problems. | Picks a plausible problem. | Provides a viable solution. | Limited or surface-level. |
| Good | Explains who users are and what they care about. | Prioritizes an important problem. | Gives multiple good options and a clear final idea. | Some connection to product or company goals. |
| Great | Shows deep empathy for user goals, context, and constraints. | Explains why the pain point exists and why it matters most. | Offers bold but concrete solutions that solve real needs. | Connects the design to long-term company strategy and market context. |

## What To Remember

Product design questions reward candidates who can combine creativity with judgment.

A strong answer should:

- Start with users, not features.
- Explain the user's context.
- Identify and prioritize pain points.
- Generate multiple possible solutions.
- Choose a solution with clear reasoning.
- Discuss tradeoffs.
- Connect the product to company and market strategy.

## 30-Second Summary

Product design questions ask you to design, improve, or discuss products. They may be called Product Sense or Design Thinking depending on the company. The interviewer is evaluating user empathy, design judgment, product vision, prioritization, creativity, and business insight. A good answer identifies users and pain points, then proposes a clear solution. A great answer goes deeper: it explains why the pain points exist, prioritizes the right problem, proposes bold but viable solutions, and ties the product direction to the company's long-term strategy and market context.
