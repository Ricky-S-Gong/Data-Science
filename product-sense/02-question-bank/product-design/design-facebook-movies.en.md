# Design Facebook Movies

## Prompt

Stephen asks Celena, a PM at Meta, to design an experience for Facebook around movies.

Before practicing, it is useful to understand what interviewers are looking for. This practice should be evaluated with a product design rubric: user understanding, problem framing, creativity, prioritization, product vision, strategic fit, and tradeoff awareness.

## Question

Design an experience for Facebook around movies.

## How To Structure This Answer

This is a broad product design question. A strong answer should not jump directly to “sell movie tickets” or “stream movies on Facebook.”

Instead, structure the answer around:

1. Facebook’s mission and product strengths.
2. The lifecycle of a movie.
3. User pain points across creation, production, and distribution.
4. Multiple product ideas.
5. Prioritization against Facebook’s strategic advantage.
6. A concrete MVP.
7. Risks, tradeoffs, and next steps.

## Strategic Starting Point

Facebook’s mission is to build community.

Therefore, any Facebook movie product should help people connect around movies, not simply consume movies alone.

A good product direction should:

- Build community.
- Use Facebook’s social graph.
- Leverage existing Facebook surfaces such as Groups, Events, News Feed, Fundraisers, and Watch.
- Address a real movie-related pain point.
- Be feasible as an MVP.
- Avoid entering a market where Facebook has no clear advantage.

## Movie Lifecycle

Start by thinking about the lifecycle of a movie.

| Stage | What Happens | Possible Facebook Opportunity |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Creation | Script writing, filming, casting, acting, creative collaboration. | Help creators find collaborators and make original content together. |
| Production | Financing, production planning, fan support, pre-release momentum. | Help fans crowdfund movies they want to see. |
| Distribution | Theatrical release, streaming, trailers, tickets, watch events. | Help users discover movies, buy tickets, watch with friends, or participate in watch parties. |

This framing helps avoid a narrow answer. Movies are not only about watching. They include creation, funding, discovery, distribution, and social experiences.

## Option 1: Collaborative Movie Creation

One idea is to help users collaborate and create original movie content together on Facebook.

Analogies:

- YouTube Symphony Orchestra.
- HitRecord by Joseph Gordon-Levitt.

The core idea:

- People with different talents can collaborate to create a movie.
- Writers, actors, editors, musicians, and directors can find one another.
- Facebook Groups can help people organize.
- Facebook Watch or video surfaces can distribute the final product.

### User Pain Point

Creators who are not already in the movie industry often struggle to:

- Find collaborators.
- Build a team.
- Coordinate creative work.
- Distribute the final project.
- Reach an audience.

### Why Facebook Could Help

Facebook is a social network with existing community infrastructure. It can help people find each other and organize around shared creative goals.

### Weakness

This idea likely requires a lot of offline work that Facebook cannot fully facilitate.

Challenges include:

- Creative coordination is complex.
- Production work happens outside the platform.
- Quality control is hard.
- It may be difficult to build a focused MVP.
- The path from collaboration to completed movie is long.

## Option 2: Movie Crowdfunding

Another idea is crowdfunding for movies.

Facebook already has a Fundraisers product that helps users raise money for nonprofits, personal causes, and community needs. A movie-focused version could help fans support films they want to exist.

Existing proof points:

- The mainstream crowdfunded movie *Veronica Mars* was funded on Kickstarter.
- Kickstarter and Indiegogo already show that crowdfunding can work as a business.

### User Pain Point

Movie fans often have no direct way to support the creation of movies they care about.

Creators also struggle to:

- Find early supporters.
- Validate demand.
- Build a fan community before release.
- Raise initial production funding.

### Why Facebook Could Help

Facebook has:

- Existing Fundraisers infrastructure.
- Social sharing through News Feed.
- Groups for fan communities.
- Events for launches, Q&A sessions, and screenings.
- Watch for trailers and promotional clips.
- Friend-of-friend virality.

Compared with Kickstarter and Indiegogo, Facebook is less niche and has a much broader social graph.

## Option 3: Movie Ticketing

Another distribution idea is movie ticketing.

Facebook could help users:

- Discover upcoming movies.
- See which friends are interested.
- Find people to watch movies with.
- RSVP to movie showings.
- Buy tickets.
- Coordinate group plans.

### User Pain Point

People who enjoy movies often have a hard time finding someone to go with.

Discovery is also fragmented:

- Trailers are shared in News Feed.
- Friends may like movie pages or events.
- Theaters may promote showings.
- Users may watch trailers through Facebook Watch.

### Why Facebook Could Help

Facebook has an advantage over Fandango because it can use social discovery.

Examples:

- Friends liked a trailer.
- Friends are interested in a movie event.
- A local theater promoted a showing.
- A user watched a trailer and showed intent.
- A group wants to organize a movie outing.

### Weakness

Fandango is already widely used for ticketing. Facebook may still have an advantage in social planning, but ticketing itself is not a uniquely Facebook-native problem.

## Option 4: Streaming Movies On Facebook Watch

Another distribution idea is showing movies on Facebook Watch.

This would compete with:

- YouTube Movies.
- Netflix.
- Hulu.
- Amazon Prime Video.
- Other streaming services.

### User Pain Point

Users often struggle to discover relevant content. Facebook has a lot of data about users’ interests and could recommend movies.

Facebook could also create live watch parties as a differentiating social feature.

### Weakness

This option has the highest barriers.

Reasons:

- Existing Facebook video behavior is often shorter-form.
- Facebook has many mobile users, and full-length movie watching may not fit the dominant use case.
- Streaming is extremely competitive.
- Original content is expensive.
- Film distribution rights are complex and often tied up with other services.
- It would take significant time and money to build differentiation.

Facebook could potentially differentiate through shorter original movie content, but this would still require major investment.

## Prioritization

To choose the final product, return to the original goal: build community around movies in a way that fits Facebook.

Prioritize using these criteria:

- Community-building potential.
- Strength of movie-related user pain point.
- Ability to leverage existing Facebook products.
- Feasibility of an MVP.
- Competitive intensity.
- Strategic fit with Facebook’s mission.

| Idea | Community Fit | Facebook Advantage | MVP Feasibility | Competition | Verdict |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Collaborative movie creation | Strong | Groups and Watch can help. | Low to medium because offline production is hard. | Medium | Interesting, but hard to MVP. |
| Movie crowdfunding | Strong | Fundraisers, News Feed, Groups, Events, Watch. | High because Fundraisers already exists. | Medium, mostly niche platforms. | Best option. |
| Movie ticketing | Medium to strong | Social graph helps coordination. | Medium | High because Fandango is common. | Good option, but less differentiated. |
| Streaming movies on Watch | Medium | Watch parties and recommendations. | Low | Very high. | Rule out for MVP. |

## Final Product Choice

I would build a Facebook movie crowdfunding product.

This best satisfies the criteria:

- It builds community.
- It helps fans support movies they want to see.
- It helps creators validate and fund demand.
- It leverages existing Facebook Fundraisers.
- It can cross-promote across News Feed, Groups, Events, and Watch.
- It is more differentiated than general ticketing.
- It is more feasible than full streaming or collaborative production.

## MVP: Facebook Movie Fundraisers

The MVP would be similar to the existing Facebook Fundraisers product, but with a special format for movies.

### Creator Flow

A creator starts a movie fundraiser and fills out:

- Movie title.
- Storyline.
- Genre.
- Team or cast information.
- Trailer or concept video.
- Funding goal.
- Timeline.
- Use of funds.
- Reward tiers.

### Fan Experience

Fans can view:

- Movie concept.
- Trailer or teaser.
- Creator profile.
- Funding progress.
- Community discussion.
- Donation tiers.
- Social proof from friends who supported the project.

### Reward Tiers

Different donation sizes could unlock:

- Free movie tickets.
- Early digital access.
- Behind-the-scenes content.
- Credits mention.
- Live Q&A with creators.
- Meet-the-actors experiences.
- Invitations to screenings or related events.

### Distribution And Virality

Once live, the creator can share the fundraiser to:

- News Feed.
- Profile.
- Groups.
- Movie-related Events.
- Watch pages with trailers.

Facebook can also cross-promote:

- To friends of supporters.
- In film-related groups.
- Near relevant local events.
- Around trailers or Watch videos.

### Community Layer

To emphasize Facebook’s mission, the product should include community features:

- Q&A with the fundraiser.
- Discussion threads.
- Related Facebook Groups.
- Related Events.
- Fan polls about creative choices.
- Updates as the movie progresses.

The goal is not only to collect money. The goal is to help a community form around a movie before it exists.

## Why Not The Other Options

### Why Not Streaming Movies On Facebook

I would rule this out because:

- It has the highest effort.
- Competition is intense.
- Rights acquisition is difficult.
- It does not build community as directly as other ideas.
- It does not address a unique Facebook user pain point as strongly.

### Why Not Collaborative Creation First

The creation and collaboration idea is compelling, but it is hard to MVP.

Reasons:

- Much of movie production happens offline.
- Collaboration quality is hard to manage.
- It requires many different creator workflows.
- The end-to-end path is long.

Facebook could eventually support creator collaboration, but crowdfunding is a more focused starting point.

### Why Not Ticketing First

Movie ticketing is also strong, especially because Facebook can help users find friends to go with.

However:

- Fandango is already ubiquitous.
- Ticketing is transactional.
- Crowdfunding creates deeper community and creator-fan engagement.
- Facebook’s Fundraisers product gives crowdfunding a faster MVP path.

## Success Metrics

Primary metrics:

- Number of movie fundraisers created.
- Percentage of fundraisers reaching funding goals.
- Total dollars raised.
- Number of unique supporters.
- Supporter-to-share conversion rate.

Community metrics:

- Comments and discussions per fundraiser.
- Q&A participation.
- Group joins from fundraiser pages.
- Event RSVPs created from fundraiser pages.
- Repeat supporters across movie fundraisers.

Creator metrics:

- Creator retention.
- Number of updates posted by creators.
- Completion rate from draft fundraiser to launched fundraiser.

Guardrails:

- Fraud reports.
- Refund requests.
- Low-quality or misleading campaigns.
- Donor dissatisfaction.
- Policy violations.

## Risks And Tradeoffs

Key risks:

- Fraudulent or unrealistic movie campaigns.
- Users funding projects that never get completed.
- Intellectual property disputes.
- Low-quality campaigns hurting trust.
- Donation fatigue.
- Regulatory or payment compliance issues.

Mitigations:

- Creator verification.
- Clear funding milestones.
- Transparent use-of-funds section.
- Refund or dispute policy.
- Campaign quality guidelines.
- Trust and safety review for large campaigns.

## 30-Second Answer

I would start from Facebook’s mission to build community and evaluate the movie lifecycle: creation, production, and distribution. I considered collaborative movie creation, crowdfunding, ticketing, and streaming through Watch. I would choose movie crowdfunding because it builds community, solves a real creator and fan pain point, leverages Facebook Fundraisers, and can MVP faster than streaming or full creative collaboration. The MVP would let creators launch movie-specific fundraisers with a storyline, trailer, funding goal, reward tiers, Q&A, and community updates, then distribute them through News Feed, Groups, Events, and Watch. I would measure success through fundraisers created, funding goal completion, dollars raised, unique supporters, community engagement, and guardrails like fraud reports and refund requests.
